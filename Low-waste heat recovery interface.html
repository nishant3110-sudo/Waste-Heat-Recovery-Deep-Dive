    Understanding Low-Enthalpy Heat

    Low-enthalpy heat has high entropy and consequently low exergy‚Äîmeaning the capacity to perform useful work is fundamentally limited by thermodynamics, even when total energy is substantial.

    What is Exergy? (Quality vs. Quantity)

    Consider this: A massive swimming pool at 30¬∞C contains far more total energy (enthalpy) than a small cup of boiling water at 100¬∞C due to its sheer mass. However, you can't efficiently use the pool water to cook an egg or spin a turbine. The boiling water has higher Exergy because its temperature is significantly higher than the surroundings. Exergy measures the useful work potential created by this temperature difference. In waste heat recovery, we hunt for Exergy‚Äîthe ability to do work‚Äîrather than just total heat content.

    Thermodynamic Cycles in Waste Heat Recovery

    The efficiency of converting waste heat into power depends heavily on the thermodynamic cycle employed. Different cycles are optimized for different temperature ranges and applications.

    Organic Rankine Cycle (ORC)

    [Image of Rankine cycle T-s diagram]
    • Type: Vapor power cycle
    • Temp Range: 80-350¬∞C
    • Efficiency: 10-25%
    • Key Feature: Uses organic fluid with lower boiling point than water.
    Key Players: Turboden, Ormat, Climeon

    Kalina Cycle

    [Image of Kalina cycle schematic]
    • Type: Variable composition cycle
    • Temp Range: 100-400¬∞C
    • Efficiency: 12-30% (Higher than ORC)
    • Key Feature: Ammonia-water mixture allows variable boiling temp (glide).
    Key Players: Kalina Power Ltd.

    Stirling Cycle

    [Image of Stirling cycle P-V diagram]
    • Type: Closed-cycle regenerative
    • Temp Range: Wide range
    • Efficiency: High theoretical (approaches Carnot)
    • Key Feature: External combustion/heat source, quiet operation.
    Key Players: Qnergy (Remote Power)

    Supercritical CO2 Brayton

    [Image of Brayton cycle T-s diagram]
    • Type: Gas power cycle (Supercritical)
    • Temp Range: >400¬∞C (High Grade)
    • Efficiency: >40%
    • Key Feature: Compact turbomachinery due to high fluid density.
    Key Players: Echogen Power Systems

    The theoretical upper limit of efficiency for any heat engine. No real engine can achieve this due to friction and irreversible processes.

    Ammonia Corrosion Management

    The primary challenge in Kalina systems is the corrosive nature of the ammonia-water working fluid, particularly towards copper-based alloys.

    Avoid

    • Copper and Copper Alloys (rapidly corroded by ammonia)
    • Zinc and Brass fittings

    Required Materials

    • Carbon Steel: Adequate for lower temperature/concentration components.
    • Stainless Steel: Mandatory for high temp/pressure zones.

    Lithium Bromide (LiBr)

    Highly corrosive to carbon steel in the presence of oxygen.

    Management Strategy:
    • Operate under deep vacuum to exclude oxygen.
    • Inhibitors: Lithium nitrate, lithium molybdate.
    • Promote stable magnetite (Fe3O4) protective layer.

    Ammonia-Water

    Corrosion of steel generates hydrogen gas, which degrades vacuum performance.

    Management Strategy:
    • Use soluble silicon compounds (sodium silicate) as inhibitors.
    • Regular chemical analysis to maintain inhibitor concentration.

    Solar Steam Generation for EOR

    Amal West Field, Oman (GlassPoint) | Commissioned 2012 (Pilot), 2015 (Miraah announced)

    TRL 9

    The Hybrid Model

    Combines solar thermal steam generation with traditional fuel-based methods (Boiler or Cogeneration). Solar provides low-cost steam during daylight hours, while fuel-based systems provide baseload and night-time capacity.

    • Solar Cost: ~$17/ton steam
    • Gas Boiler Cost: ~$27/ton (at $6/MMBtu gas)
    • Fuel Break-Even: Solar beats gas when fuel >$4.50/MMBtu

    Strategic Relevance for Devon

    This model directly addresses thermal EOR needs in gas-constrained environments. It allows operators to divert valuable natural gas to higher-margin sales (e.g., LNG export) while maintaining reservoir pressure.

    Constraint: Solar fraction limited to ~80% without storage due to need for continuous night-time injection to maintain well integrity.

    To improve the accuracy of project screening, the following variables should be integrated into future iterations of this calculator:

    1

    Net vs. Gross Carbon Accounting

    Current models often use gross CO2 removal. Future models must account for upstream emissions (Scope 3) of the energy source used for capture (e.g., grid intensity vs. waste heat).

    2

    Dynamic Marginal Pricing

    Replace static fuel costs with dynamic curves that reflect the opportunity cost of fuel (e.g., LNG export parity prices) rather than subsidized domestic rates.

    3

    Capacity Factor & Storage Integration

    For solar/hybrid systems, explicitly model the cost impact of thermal storage hours required to meet the specific reservoir's night-time injection constraints.

    Strategic Conclusion

    A differentiated approach is required to unlock the full value of waste heat in Oil & Gas operations. There is no one-size-fits-all solution; success requires precision, financial strategy, and corporate alignment.

    1 Midstream Imperative

    Midstream operators should pursue large-scale, high-impact projects at centralized facilities. The combination of MW-scale ORC systems for baseload power generation and absorption chillers for turbine inlet cooling can transform compressor stations into dual revenue centers.

    • Maximize economies of scale
    • Leverage centralized infrastructure

    2 Upstream Imperative

    Upstream operators must prioritize modularity, reliability, and learning value. Building capabilities now extends beyond immediate ROI to enable future opportunities like geothermal development. TEG deployments and modular ORC systems create immediate value while developing competency.

    • Focus on distributed assets
    • Build thermal competency

    Five Pillars of Success

    1. Technology Matching

    Precise technology-to-application alignment based on specific heat source characteristics and operational constraints.

    2. Material Selection

    Treating materials as a primary cost driver from the start, not an afterthought, to ensure longevity and performance.

    3. Financial Engineering

    Leveraging the full incentive stack (ITC, state programs, carbon credits) to meet internal hurdle rates.

    4. Strategic Alignment

    Alignment with broader corporate goals like ESG targets and capacity building, beyond just efficiency.

    5. Commercial Pragmatism

    Recognizing limited VC engagement by prioritizing established vendors or innovative financing structures.
